scRNA-seq QC Guide

A Detailed Guide to
Quality Control (QC) in Single-Cell RNA Sequencing Data Analysis

In the scRNA-seq data analysis workflow, Quality Control (QC) is an extremely critical first step. Due to limitations in single-cell capture technology and differences in cell status, raw data often contain a large amount of useless noise, such as dead cells, broken droplets, and doublets.

Why is this important? Without rigorous QC, these low-quality data can severely interfere with downstream dimensionality reduction, clustering, and differential expression analysis, and may even lead to completely incorrect biological conclusions.

I. Core Cell-Level QC Metrics

Cell-level filtering aims to remove poor-quality cell barcodes. Below is an interactive simulator that allows you to experience how different threshold settings affect the dataset. Try dragging the sliders below.

1. Number of Genes nFeature_RNA

The total number of detected genes in a single cell.
Too low (<200): Cell rupture or empty droplets.
Too high: Suggestive of doublets.

2. Total UMI Count nCount_RNA

The total number of measured transcript molecules. It should show a strong positive correlation with gene count.
Abnormally high or low values are often associated with empty droplets or multiple cells.

3. Mitochondrial Percentage MT%

The most important metric for evaluating cell viability and health status. When cells rupture, mRNA is lost, but mitochondrial RNA tends to remain.
Common threshold: remove cells with greater than 5%–20%.

II. Gene-Level QC

In addition to filtering cells, we also need to remove genes that have little analytical value in order to reduce computational burden and statistical noise.

Filtering extremely lowly expressed genes:
A common practice is to remove genes expressed in fewer than 3 cells. These genes are highly likely to be the result of sequencing error or to have expression levels too low to provide meaningful statistical significance in downstream analysis.

Other Supporting Cell Metrics

  • 🧬
    Ribosomal Ratio A low ribosomal ratio sometimes suggests poor cell quality, though this metric is less universally useful than mitochondrial percentage (typically based on genes beginning with RPS/RPL).
  • 🩸
    Hemoglobin Ratio For blood or highly vascularized tissues, cells with high HBA/HBB expression are often filtered out unless red blood cells are the focus of the study.

III. Advanced QC: Handling Technical Artifacts

Basic threshold filtering cannot solve every problem. Even after removing extreme values, difficult-to-identify artifacts may still remain in the dataset. Modern analysis should therefore include the following advanced steps:

1. Doublets

Two cells are encapsulated in the same droplet. On UMAP, they may form artificial "transitional states." A simple upper threshold for nCount/nFeature cannot completely remove doublets composed of two small cells.

Solution:
Use algorithms to simulate doublet expression profiles and calculate a probability score for each cell (Doublet Score).
Common tools: DoubletFinder, Scrublet, scDblFinder

2. Ambient RNA (Soup)

RNA released from ruptured cells enters the suspension and is co-encapsulated into droplets. This can cause cells that should not express a certain gene (for example, T cells) to falsely appear to express it (for example, a hepatocyte-specific gene).

Solution:
Estimate the ambient RNA distribution and subtract this contamination from the expression matrix.
Common tools: SoupX, CellBender

IV. QC Threshold Strategies and Best Practices

Strategy Type Fixed Thresholds (Traditional) Adaptive Thresholds (Current Best Practice)
Concept Uses subjective hard cutoffs (for example, MT% < 5%) Determined based on the data distribution (data-driven)
Common Method Conventional thresholds from the literature Median Absolute Deviation (MAD): median + 3 × MAD
Disadvantage / Advantage Highly error-prone in multi-sample integration or special tissue analyses. More robust to extreme values and better able to adapt to quality differences between samples (for example, isOutlier() in scater).

💡 Core Expert Recommendations

  • 1

    There is no universal threshold: QC must be adjusted according to your species, tissue type (for example, cardiomyocytes naturally have higher mitochondrial content), and sequencing platform. Always inspect violin plots and scatter plots.

  • 2

    Joint distributions are more informative than single metrics: As shown in the interactive plot above, good cells should lie roughly along the diagonal; cells deviating from this pattern are often problematic.

  • 3

    Be slightly permissive rather than over-filtering: Excessively strict filtering may remove rare cell subpopulations, such as quiescent cells.

  • 4

    Treat QC as an iterative process: After initial permissive clustering, if a cluster shows high mitochondrial percentage and lacks specific markers, you can return to the QC step and remove it.
