scRNA-seq QC Guide

Understanding Quality Control (QC)
in Single-Cell RNA Sequencing Analysis

In scRNA-seq analysis, quality control (QC) is one of the most important first steps. Because of technical limitations in single-cell capture and biological differences in cell state, raw data often contain substantial noise, including dead cells, broken droplets, empty droplets, and doublets.

Why does this matter? Without careful QC, low-quality data can distort downstream dimensionality reduction, clustering, and differential expression analyses, and may ultimately lead to misleading biological interpretations.

I. Core Cell-Level QC Metrics

Cell-level filtering aims to remove poor-quality cell barcodes. The interactive simulator below allows you to explore how different threshold settings influence the dataset. Try adjusting the sliders.

1. Number of Detected Genes nFeature_RNA

The total number of genes detected in a single cell.
Too low (<200): often suggests broken cells or empty droplets.
Too high: may indicate potential doublets.

2. Total UMI Count nCount_RNA

The total number of transcript molecules detected in a cell. This metric is usually strongly positively correlated with gene count.
Abnormally low or high values often reflect empty droplets or multiplets.

3. Mitochondrial Read Percentage MT%

One of the most informative indicators of cell health and viability. When cells rupture, cytoplasmic mRNA is lost more easily, whereas mitochondrial RNA tends to remain.
A common rule is to remove cells with more than 5%–20%.

II. Gene-Level QC

In addition to filtering cells, we also remove genes that carry little analytical value. This helps reduce computational burden and suppress statistical noise.

Filtering very lowly expressed genes:
A common approach is to discard genes detected in fewer than 3 cells. Such genes are often sequencing artifacts or too sparsely expressed to contribute meaningfully to downstream statistical analysis.

Additional Supporting Metrics

  • 🧬
    Ribosomal Gene Ratio A low ribosomal fraction can sometimes suggest poor cell quality, although this metric is less universally informative than mitochondrial percentage. It is usually calculated from genes beginning with RPS or RPL.
  • 🩸
    Hemoglobin Gene Ratio In blood-rich or highly vascularized tissues, cells with excessive HBA or HBB expression are often removed unless red blood cells are part of the biological question.

III. Advanced QC: Technical Artifacts

Simple threshold-based filtering does not solve every problem. Even after removing obvious outliers, difficult-to-detect technical artifacts may remain in the dataset. Modern scRNA-seq analysis usually includes the following additional steps:

1. Doublets

Doublets occur when two cells are captured within the same droplet. In downstream embedding, they may appear as artificial “intermediate” or “transitional” states. A simple upper cutoff for nCount or nFeature cannot reliably eliminate all doublets, especially those formed by two relatively small cells.

Typical solution:
Use dedicated algorithms that model doublet-like expression profiles and assign each cell a doublet probability score.
Common tools: DoubletFinder, Scrublet, scDblFinder

2. Ambient RNA Contamination

Ambient RNA arises when RNA released from ruptured cells contaminates the suspension and is captured together with intact cells. As a result, a cell may appear to express genes that it should not biologically express.

Typical solution:
Estimate the ambient RNA profile and computationally subtract the contamination signal from the expression matrix.
Common tools: SoupX, CellBender

💡 Key Practical Recommendations

  • 1

    There is no universal cutoff: QC should always be adjusted according to species, tissue type, and sequencing platform. For example, some tissues naturally show higher mitochondrial content. Always inspect the actual distribution with violin plots and scatter plots.

  • 2

    Joint distributions are more informative than any single metric: As shown in the interactive plot above, high-quality cells often follow a diagonal trend between nCount and nFeature. Cells deviating strongly from that pattern are often problematic.

  • 3

    Avoid over-filtering: Very stringent thresholds may remove rare but biologically meaningful populations, such as quiescent or metabolically distinct cells.

  • 4

    Treat QC as an iterative process: After an initial round of permissive filtering and clustering, you may identify clusters with high mitochondrial content and no clear biological markers, and then return to refine the QC step.
