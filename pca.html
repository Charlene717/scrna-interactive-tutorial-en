Principal Component Analysis (PCA) in Single-Cell RNA Sequencing

When working with single-cell datasets containing more than 20,000 gene dimensions, PCA serves as both an essential information compressor and a noise filter. It helps us extract real biological structure from otherwise overwhelming complexity.

1. Why Do We Need PCA in scRNA-seq?

Human and mouse cells each contain expression information for more than 20,000 genes, creating an extremely high-dimensional data space. Direct analysis of these data presents three major challenges:

🌌

Curse of Dimensionality

In very high-dimensional space, measuring distances or similarity between cells becomes increasingly difficult and often loses both mathematical stability and biological meaning.

💻

Computational Burden

Running clustering or nonlinear dimensionality reduction directly on more than 20,000 genes requires substantial memory and computational time.

🧹

Noise Reduction

scRNA-seq data contain extensive technical noise. PCA helps extract the main axes that reflect true biological signal, while pushing much of the random noise into later components.

2. Core Concepts and Principles of PCA

PCA is a linear dimensionality reduction method. Its core idea is to identify directions that maximize the variance in the data. Through PCA, an original 20,000-dimensional dataset can often be summarized into the first 10–50 principal components (PCs).

PC1 (First Principal Component)

Captures the largest source of variation in the dataset, for example the broad difference between immune and epithelial cells.

PC2 (Second Principal Component)

Orthogonal to PC1 and captures the next-largest source of remaining variation.

Later PCs...

Capture progressively smaller amounts of variation, and later components are increasingly likely to reflect technical noise.

4. How Do We Decide How Many PCs to Use?

Keeping too few PCs may lose biologically meaningful substructure, while keeping too many may introduce noise. In practice, we often use the Elbow Plot or JackStraw analysis to guide this choice.

Example Elbow Plot

Look for the turning point where the curve drops steeply and then begins to flatten. This is often used as a reasonable estimate for the number of PCs to retain, commonly somewhere between 10 and 20.

5. Downstream Applications and 6. Limitations

Once the retained PCs are chosen, for example the first 20, this lower-dimensional representation becomes the basis for most downstream analyses.

➔ Downstream Applications of PCA

Cell Clustering

Algorithms such as Louvain or Leiden typically build a k-nearest neighbor graph using the selected PCs and then group cells based on that structure.

Nonlinear Dimensionality Reduction and Visualization (UMAP / t-SNE)

UMAP and t-SNE are mainly used for two-dimensional visualization. Their input is usually not the original expression matrix, but rather the PCA-reduced representation. This both accelerates computation and improves visualization quality.

Trajectory Inference

Many developmental trajectory tools, such as pseudotime methods, also rely on PCA space to estimate relationships between cellular states.

⚠️ Inherent Limitations of PCA

📈 ➔ 🧬

Limitations of the Linear Assumption

PCA can only capture linear relationships between variables. However, biological systems, including gene regulatory networks and differentiation trajectories, are often highly nonlinear.

This is why PCA is typically not used as the final two-dimensional visualization tool. It cannot unfold complex nonlinear manifolds well, which is why methods such as UMAP or t-SNE are used afterward for nonlinear embedding.
