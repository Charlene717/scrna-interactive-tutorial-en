scRNA-seq Analysis Guide

Phase 1

The Purpose of Clustering Analysis

In a typical scRNA-seq experiment, we obtain high-dimensional gene expression profiles for thousands of cells. Clustering is the key step that groups together cells with similar transcriptomic features. This section outlines why clustering is such a central part of single-cell analysis.

🔍

Identify Cellular Heterogeneity

Reveal the distinct functional cell populations that exist within what may initially appear to be a single tissue sample.

✨

Discover Novel Cell Types

Use unsupervised learning to identify previously undefined or very rare cellular subpopulations that may not be evident through traditional biological experiments.

🏷️

Assign Cell-Type Identity

After clustering, combine the results with known marker genes to assign biologically meaningful labels to each previously unknown cell group.

Phase 2

Essential Preprocessing Before Clustering

Clustering is not performed directly on the raw expression matrix. Raw single-cell data contain substantial technical noise and redundant dimensions. This section summarizes the three standard preprocessing steps required before the clustering algorithm is applied.

A

A Normalization and Transformation

To reduce physical differences in sequencing depth across cells:

  • Library-size normalization: Scale the total expression of each cell to a constant value, for example 10,000.
  • Log transformation: Apply log(x + 1) to reduce skewness and stabilize variance.
B

B Highly Variable Gene Selection

Not all genes are equally informative for distinguishing cell types, especially housekeeping genes.

We usually select 2,000 to 3,000 highly variable genes (HVGs) using statistical criteria. These genes show the largest expression differences across cells, reduce noise, and retain the most relevant information for clustering.
C

C Dimensionality Reduction

This step addresses the curse of dimensionality in high-dimensional data.

📉

PCA (Principal Component Analysis) is used to project thousands of gene dimensions into the top 30–50 principal components that capture the most relevant structure. Clustering is usually performed in this PCA-reduced space.

Phase 3

Comparison of Major Clustering Algorithms

Once dimensionality reduction is complete, the core mathematical grouping step begins. This section compares two of the most commonly discussed clustering strategies in single-cell analysis.

Most commonly used / Current standard

Graph-Based Clustering

Graph-based community detection

This is the default core strategy used in major toolkits such as Seurat (R) and Scanpy (Python).

Three-step logic:

  1. 1
    Construct a KNN Graph

    Compute distances between cells in PCA space and connect each cell to its K nearest neighbors.

  2. 2
    Weight the Edges

    Use a Shared Nearest Neighbor (SNN) strategy to refine the edge weights between cells.

  3. 3
    Detect Communities

    Apply Louvain or Leiden algorithms to partition the graph into communities. Leiden generally produces more well-connected clusters.

K-means Clustering

Traditional centroid-based clustering

This is a classic algorithm in traditional machine learning, but it faces several limitations in single-cell analysis.

⚙️ Core Idea

The user must specify the expected number of clusters K in advance, and the algorithm iteratively moves centroids to assign cells into groups.

⚠️ Why It Is Less Common in scRNA-seq

  • Biologically, the true number of cell populations is often unknown in advance.
  • K-means assumes clusters are roughly convex and similarly sized, which makes it poorly suited for irregularly shaped or highly imbalanced single-cell populations.

Phase 6 & 7

Biological Interpretation and Common Pitfalls

Clustering is only a mathematical grouping step. The final and most important task is to assign biological meaning to those groups through annotation, while also recognizing the common technical pitfalls that can distort clustering results. This section summarizes both annotation strategies and major warning signs.

How Do We Assign Biological Meaning to Clusters?

1. Differential Expression Analysis

Compare a target cluster against all other cells to identify genes with significantly elevated expression, that is, cluster-specific marker genes.

2. Automated Annotation Tools

Use tools such as SingleR or Azimuth to compare your clusters against high-quality reference datasets.

3. Expert Manual Review

This remains the gold standard. A domain expert reviews the marker genes and compares them with known literature to confirm the biological identity of each cluster.

Warning Zone: Common Pitfalls in Clustering Analysis

✂️ Over-clustering

What it looks like: A single biological cell type is artificially split into multiple separate clusters.
Why it happens: The resolution parameter is set too high, or technical noise such as subtle batch effects or sequencing-depth differences is misinterpreted as biological heterogeneity.

🧪 Batch Effects

What it looks like: Clusters reflect sample origin, such as Hospital A versus Hospital B, rather than true biological cell types.
How to address it: If multiple experimental batches are combined, dedicated integration or batch-correction methods, such as Seurat Integration or Harmony, should be applied before clustering.

👯 Doublets

What it looks like: A small bridging cluster appears between two major groups on a UMAP plot and cannot be explained by the marker genes of a single cell type.
Why it happens: Two different cells were accidentally captured in the same droplet, creating a mixed expression profile. These should be identified and removed with tools such as DoubletFinder.
